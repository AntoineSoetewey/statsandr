Why do I blog? 5 benefits of sharing your code

My blog statsandr.com was launched in December 2019. Although 9 months of writing is short compared to others, I can already say that it’s been an incredible and very enriching adventure!

With 45 articles published, I have seen many advantages of sharing my code through a blog. In this article, I highlight xxx of them with the hope that it will give some ideas and incentives to some of you.

1 Learn by writing

Obviously, I am learning mainly about R, statistics and data science as they are the main topics of the blog. However, I never thought that I would also learn so much about: + web development and SEO (which are increasingly important skills nowadays) + project management + writing and communicating + marketing/public relation/brand management + having a side hustle + etc.

Maintaining a blog teaches me essential skills that are usually taught in jobs. With a blog, you are responsible of everything from the content to readers’ inquiries, similar to an employee in charge of a project and who has to deal and communicate with end users.

Don’t get me wrong, I do not think that a blog can replace a job in terms of personal development, but it definitely helps to learn a broad range of important skills. Reading books and completing data science online courses are other examples to gain skills in addition to a job, but a blog tends to be more diverse and is thus, in my opinion, more beneficial.

2 Get feedback

Get feedback, questions and constructive critics from more experienced users. This greatly helps in correcting typos, bugs and mistakes present in my code. You will be amazed to see that, although you spent countless hours to check your code, there will always be someone who will spot a typo that you missed.

3 Personal note to remind my future self

How many times you searched for a piece of code in the folders of your computer, to finally look for the answer on Google because you could not remember for which project you wrote that piece of code? It happened to me every day.

With blog posts organized by topics, it now takes me much less time to find the piece of code that I wrote months ago.

4 Contribute to the open source community

I have learned so much and I keep learning everyday about R thanks to great resources made available for free by developers and scientists who believe in open source and free materials.

Making my code and articles freely available through a blog is in some sense, my way of: + “paying back” people who helped me to learn and thanks to whom I stand where I am now, and + pay in advance the many more from who I will learn in the future

5 Stay humble

Since launching my blog, I discovered plenty of other blogs (mainly through R-bloggers and R Weekly which are two great blog aggregators). And the more I see new things about different topics, the more I realize that I actually do not know much.

This reminds me to stay humble.

6 Learn to be less perfectionist

No matter how much time you spend on your blog, remember that everything cannot always be perfect. As a perfectionist, I cannot deny that I would love everything to run perfectly in all aspects of my life.

At the time of writing my first articles, my tendency for perfectionism was sometimes so prevalent that it was a real weakness: I could spend several minutes thinking whether a comma between two words was needed or not (which would of course make no difference at all). However, at some point, improving something takes so much time and energy that the added value is not as large as the added value you could obtain by devoting your time in creating something else.

This gets even worse as you keep learning something new every day. An article which seemed to be perfect in the past may no longer be viewed as perfect today, so you want to change or add a small detail in all your articles. I am not saying you should not edit a blog post, it is even recommended! Nonetheless, I advise you to edit it only if it really adds a significant value to it.

With a blog you will thus gradually learn to put your energy and time (which are limited resources and seem to be scarcer and scarcer every year) where it is most productive. You will learn to prioritize.

7 Be confronted to other views and perspectives

Through my PhD in statistics, I am used to work and collaborate with researchers who all have more or less the same background as me.

By sharing my code with scientists from diverse backgrounds and coming from all over the world, it allows me to consider other perspectives and practice open-mindedness, which in turn helps me to stay curious.

8 Build connections

By sharing your practices in such a specific field, you sometimes come across people who actually work on the same topic or have similar goals. This was the case with: * Rees Morrison with the collection of top R resources on Coronavirus * Renan Xavier Cortes with the package xxx

Although we do not know each other and we never met in person, I sometimes feel that I know someone because we share something in common (can be a common interest, practice, goal or view).

9 Redirect students to avoid answering the same questions hundreds of times. + helping people to gain a better understanding of statistics and its potential applications in R.

If this blog is useful to at least some people in demystifying statistics, my goal will be reached.

Thanks for reading. I hope this article answered questions such as “Why do you have a blog?” or “What is the purpose of it?”, and who knows, gave you the motivation to start your own blog. If you are still undecided, I truly recommend to do it and remember that the hardest part is to start, the rest will follow if you are patient and passionate about a topic.

As always, if you have a question or a suggestion related to the topic covered in this article, please add it as a comment so other readers can benefit from the discussion.
